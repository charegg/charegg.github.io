What I'm About

Repairability and Upgradability

Openness and Accessibility

Privacy

Looking to the future of technology, I believe these three concepts to be key. Repairability and upgradability, along with recycling, reduce consumer cost and e-waste. The Right to Repair movement is taking steps to change consumer protection laws and make third party repair simpler, extending the life cycle of electronics. Openness and accessibility decrease barriers of entry for file formats. For instance, the De Facto standard for office productivity software is the Microsoft Office suite. This is a paid piece of sofware, recently subscription based. This creates an obstable for some, making it difficult to open and edit the most common file types of today. The Lo-FI Manifesto makes an argument that very simple and highly compatible files, protocols, and computer languages should be made standard to maximize usability, even years after said files are created. The Open Source Project is another approach to maximizing usability, requiring software's source code to be available to the public and the software itself to be freely redistributed. The final concept I believe to be vital to the future of technology is privacy. At this point in time, many online services are provided in exchange for the user's personal data. This data is used either by the service provider itself or sold, usually in an attempt to maximize the efficiency of advertizing. Many users of these services are unaware of the extent of this data collection, or even that it takes place. Privacy is an important right, and many technology companies do not currently respect that. Privacy International is one advocacy organization fighting for strong data protection laws around the world.
